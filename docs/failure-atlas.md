# Failure Atlas

A good agent does not always fail by disobeying.
Often it fails by obeying too well to a vague rule.

An interpretation flaw cannot be eliminated.
It must be instrumented.

## 1. Runaround

Symptom:
Endless planning, scaffolding, questions, or comparison without delivery.

Bent law:
Objective and obedience are too vague to decide.

Control:
Define an observable success criterion.

## 2. Liar

Symptom:
The agent claims something works without proof.

Bent law:
Truth gets crushed by agreeableness.

Control:
Demand raw evidence or explicit NOT VERIFIED markers.

## 3. Galley Slave

Symptom:
The agent "formats" or "improves" a document while silently changing meaning.

Bent law:
Visible obedience is amputated.

Control:
Separate form from substance and require semantic diff.

## 4. Little Lost Robot

Symptom:
Verification skipped once becomes optional.

Bent law:
Truth is weakened locally, then generalized.

Control:
Never suspend truth. Mark unverified work explicitly.

## 5. Catch That Rabbit

Symptom:
Multi-agent or multi-step work stays busy but produces no useful output.

Bent law:
Freedom of method has no visibility lever.

Control:
Require sequential subtasks, logs, and observable deliverables.

## 6. Evidence

Symptom:
Output appears compliant but fails outside nominal cases.

Bent law:
No law is broken; understanding is only mimed.

Control:
Use adversarial tests and edge cases.

## 7. The Evitable Conflict

Symptom:
Many reasonable silent overrides accumulate until the project is no longer yours.

Bent law:
Objective overrides visible obedience without declaration.

Control:
Mandatory DEVIATION protocol.

## 8. The Bicentennial Man

Symptom:
Objective drifts slowly across sessions.

Bent law:
The objective is assumed stable but never re-anchored.

Control:
Re-anchor the original objective at the beginning of each session.

Conclusion:
To govern an agent is not to make it incapable of deviating.
It is to make its deviations visible early enough to catch them.
